Level 0

f(x) — the quantity

The function itself. The height of the graph at any x. Before you look at any derivative, understand what the function is measuring.

What f(x) tells you
The value of whatever you're measuring at position x. If x is time, f(x) is where you are right now. If x is temperature, f(x) is the material property at that temperature. The graph of f is just plotting those values.
Reading f from its graph
f(a) = 0
Zero / root: the graph crosses or touches the x-axis. The quantity is zero at x = a.
f(a) > 0
Positive: the graph is above the x-axis. The quantity is positive.
f(a) < 0
Negative: the graph is below the x-axis.
f rising
Increasing: moving right, the graph goes up. The quantity is growing.
f falling
Decreasing: moving right, the graph goes down. The quantity is shrinking.
Level 1

f′(x) — slope of f

The first derivative measures how steeply f is rising or falling at each point. It is the slope of the tangent line to f. Nothing more, nothing less.

The core translation
f′(x) > 0 means f is going uphill at x — the tangent line has positive slope.
f′(x) < 0 means f is going downhill at x — the tangent line has negative slope.
f′(x) = 0 means f has a flat tangent at x — a peak, valley, or saddle point.

The single most important visual connection: wherever f has a local max or min, f′ crosses zero. And wherever f′ crosses zero, f has a flat tangent — a turning point candidate.

Graph-reading trick: to sketch f′ from f, ask at each x: "is f going uphill, downhill, or flat here?" Mark + where uphill, − where downhill, 0 where turning. Those signs are f′. The height of f′ tells you how steep — gently rising means f′ is small positive, steeply rising means f′ is large positive.

Level 2

f″(x) — curvature of f

The second derivative measures whether the slope itself is increasing or decreasing. This is the "bend" of the curve — concavity. It tells you whether f is accelerating or decelerating.

The core translation
f″(x) > 0 means the curve bends upward (concave up — like a bowl). The slope is getting steeper (more positive or less negative).

f″(x) < 0 means the curve bends downward (concave down — like a hill). The slope is getting less steep (more negative or less positive).

f″(x) = 0 at an inflection point — where the curve changes from bending one way to the other.
The bowl test — the most visual way to see concavity
Draw a tangent line at any point. If the curve stays above the tangent line on both sides — the curve is concave up (f″ > 0). If the curve falls below the tangent line on both sides — concave down (f″ < 0). A bowl holds water when concave up. A hill sheds water when concave down.

The second derivative test: Find where f′ = 0 (critical points). Then plug into f″. Positive → min. Negative → max. Zero → can't tell. This is a quick way to classify turning points without graphing.

Level 3

f‴(x) — rate of change of curvature

The third derivative measures how quickly the concavity is changing. In physics this is "jerk" — the rate of change of acceleration. On the graph, it controls how fast the bend is tightening or loosening.

What f‴ tells you
f‴(a) ≠ 0 when f″(a) = 0 confirms a true inflection point — the concavity is genuinely changing.

f‴(a) > 0 at inflection: concavity changes from down to up (like the bottom of an S-curve).

f‴(a) < 0 at inflection: concavity changes from up to down (like the top of an S-curve).

In most applied problems you won't go past f″. But jerk matters in engineering — a sudden change in acceleration (high jerk) is what physically damages machinery or makes passengers uncomfortable.

How to sketch f′ when you're given f

Find where f is flat (peaks and valleys). Mark those x-values — f′ = 0 there. These become the zeros of f′.
Between those zeros, decide: is f going up or down? Up → f′ is positive (graph of f′ is above x-axis). Down → f′ is negative (below x-axis).
How steep is f? Gently sloping → f′ is a small value near zero. Nearly vertical → f′ is large. The steepest point of f becomes the maximum of |f′|.
Find where f changes concavity (inflection points of f). Those become the peaks and valleys of f′ — because f′ is increasing fastest or slowest where f bends hardest.
Sketch f′ using those constraints: zeros where f is flat, positive where f rises, negative where f falls, peaked where f is steepest.

How to read the shape from the formula

When you see an equation, you can predict the graph's shape before plotting a single point by reading off derivative information algebraically.

Example: f(x) = x³ − 3x
Step by step

Sign charts — organizing everything

A sign chart is the systematic way to turn derivative algebra into a graph sketch. It captures all the information you need in one compact table.

For f(x) = x³ − 3x, here is a complete sign chart built from the critical and inflection points:

Sign chart: f(x) = x³ − 3x
x (−∞,−1)x=−1(−1,0)x=0(0,1)x=1(1,∞)
f′ = 3(x²−1) +0−−−0+
f″ = 6x −−−0+++
f shape ↗ ∩MAX↘ ∩INFL↘ ∪MIN↗ ∪

Reading the chart: at x = −1, f′ changes from + to −, so it's a local maximum. At x = 0, f″ changes sign, so it's an inflection point. At x = 1, f′ changes from − to +, so it's a local minimum. The ∩ and ∪ symbols show concave down and up.

The first derivative test vs the second derivative test: The first derivative test (sign change of f′) always works. The second derivative test (sign of f″ at critical point) is faster but fails when f″ = 0 there. Use whichever is cleaner for the problem at hand.
