The Unit Circle

Every trig value comes from a point on a circle of radius 1. The x-coordinate is cos(θ), the y-coordinate is sin(θ). This is the geometric definition — not just a formula to memorize.

Fundamental definitions

sin θ = opp / hyp = y cos θ = adj / hyp = x tan θ = sin θ / cos θ = y/x

On the unit circle (r=1), hypotenuse = 1, so sin and cos are literally the y and x coordinates of the point.

Foundation of every trig derivative

Reciprocal functions

csc θ = 1/sin θ sec θ = 1/cos θ cot θ = 1/tan θ = cos θ/sin θ

These show up when you apply the quotient or chain rule to tan, cot, sec, csc. The derivatives of sec and csc require recognizing these reciprocals.

Derivatives involve these

Key angles (memorize these)

0° → sin=0, cos=1 30° → sin=1/2, cos=√3/2 45° → sin=√2/2, cos=√2/2 60° → sin=√3/2, cos=1/2 90° → sin=1, cos=0

Calc 1 integration problems love 30-60-90 and 45-45-90 triangles. You'll see them constantly in trig substitution problems.

ASTC — sign by quadrant

Q1 (0–90°): All positive Q2 (90–180°): Sin positive Q3 (180–270°): Tan positive Q4 (270–360°): Cos positive

"All Students Take Calculus" — you need this to evaluate limits and definite integrals over intervals that cross the axes. Sign errors are the most common mistake.

Sign analysis in integration

Radians vs degrees

π rad = 180° deg → rad: × π/180 rad → deg: × 180/π Arc length: s = rθ (θ in rad)

Calculus is done exclusively in radians. The derivative d/dx[sin x] = cos x is only true when x is in radians. If you use degrees, you get a factor of π/180 everywhere.

Always use radians in calculus

Inverse trig (arcsin, arccos, arctan)

arcsin: domain [−1,1], range [−π/2, π/2] arccos: domain [−1,1], range [0, π] arctan: domain (−∞,∞), range (−π/2, π/2)

These appear as antiderivatives in Calc 2. Knowing the range is critical — arcsin(1/2) = π/6 (not 5π/6), because we restrict to the principal value range.

Trig Identities

Identities hold for all valid θ. In calculus you use them to simplify derivatives, collapse integrands, and evaluate limits. Each one below has a "why it matters in calc" note.

Pythagorean identities

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

The Pythagorean theorem on the unit circle. Used to substitute sin² = 1−cos² (or vice versa) to simplify integrands, especially in trig substitution.

★ Most used in calculus
1 + tan²θ = sec²θ

Divide first identity by cos²θ. Directly explains why d/dx[tan x] = sec²x — it shows up in the derivative's result.

1 + cot²θ = csc²θ

Divide first identity by sin²θ. Matches d/dx[cot x] = −csc²x.

Double angle formulas

sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ

Replace sin(x)cos(x) with ½sin(2x) before integrating — turns a hard product into a simple integral in one step.

Integration shortcut
cos(2θ) = cos²θ − sin²θ = 2cos²θ − 1 = 1 − 2sin²θ

Three equivalent forms — you rearrange the last two into power-reducing identities below. Essential for ∫cos²x dx and ∫sin²x dx.

Power reduction key

Power-reducing identities

sin²θ = (1 − cos 2θ) / 2

From cos(2θ)=1−2sin²θ, solved for sin²θ. Without this, ∫sin²x dx is basically impossible — with it, it takes two lines.

∫sin²x dx uses this
cos²θ = (1 + cos 2θ) / 2

From cos(2θ)=2cos²θ−1, solved for cos²θ. Same idea. Also used for ∫sin²x cos²x dx by applying both identities first.

∫cos²x dx uses this

Sum & difference formulas

sin(A±B) = sinA cosB ± cosA sinB

Used to expand trig of shifted arguments, e.g. sin(x + π/4). Also how you prove d/dx[sin x] = cos x from the limit definition — expand sin(x+h) using this formula.

Proves d/dx[sin x]
cos(A±B) = cosA cosB ∓ sinA sinB

Note the ∓ (opposite sign from ±). Used with the limit definition to prove d/dx[cos x] = −sin x.

Even & odd identities

sin(−θ) = −sin θ (odd function) cos(−θ) = cos θ (even function) tan(−θ) = −tan θ (odd function)

Odd functions are symmetric about the origin: ∫from −a to a of an odd function = 0. This saves huge amounts of work in definite integrals. Even functions satisfy ∫from −a to a = 2·∫from 0 to a.

Definite integral symmetry

Product-to-sum formulas

sinA sinB = ½[cos(A−B) − cos(A+B)] cosA cosB = ½[cos(A−B) + cos(A+B)] sinA cosB = ½[sin(A+B) + sin(A−B)]

Convert products of trig functions into sums — then integrate term by term. Appears in Fourier series (a major topic in signals/CS math), orthogonality proofs, and trig integrals with different frequencies.

Limits & Foundational Identities

Limits are the engine that powers all of calculus. The derivative is defined as a limit. These special trig limits appear directly in the proofs of derivative formulas.

Squeeze theorem: cos(x) ≤ sin(x)/x ≤ 1 forces the limit to 1 as x→0

Two critical trig limits

lim(x→0) sin(x)/x = 1

This is how d/dx[sin x] = cos x is proved from scratch using the limit definition of derivative. Without it, no trig derivatives exist. Proved by squeezing between cos(x) and 1.

Proves d/dx[sin x] = cos x
lim(x→0) (cos(x)−1)/x = 0

The second limit needed to prove d/dx[cos x] = −sin x. Intuitively: cos(0) = 1 and cos barely changes near 0, so the difference quotient goes to zero.

Proves d/dx[cos x] = −sin x

Limit laws

lim [f±g] = lim f ± lim g lim [f·g] = lim f · lim g lim [f/g] = lim f / lim g (g≠0) lim [c·f] = c · lim f

These let you break complex limits apart. The proofs of the product rule, quotient rule, and sum rule for derivatives all rely directly on these limit laws.

L'Hôpital's rule

If lim f/g = 0/0 or ∞/∞: lim f(x)/g(x) = lim f'(x)/g'(x)

When direct substitution gives 0/0 or ∞/∞, differentiate numerator and denominator separately (NOT the quotient rule) then re-evaluate. Used heavily for limits involving e^x, ln(x), sin(x) combinations.

Differentiate top and bottom separately

Squeeze theorem

If g(x) ≤ f(x) ≤ h(x) and lim g = lim h = L then lim f(x) = L

How sin(x)/x → 1 is proved rigorously. Also used for oscillating functions like x²·sin(1/x): since |sin(1/x)| ≤ 1, we have −x² ≤ x²sin(1/x) ≤ x², so the limit at 0 is squeezed to 0.

Continuity

f is continuous at a if: lim(x→a) f(x) = f(a)

A function must be continuous to be differentiable. The Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT) and Mean Value Theorem (MVT) both require continuity. IVT lets you prove roots exist — used in Newton's method.

Required for MVT, IVT

Key limits involving e and ln

lim(x→∞) (1 + 1/x)^x = e ≈ 2.718

The definition of e. This is why compound interest converges to e^(rt) with continuous compounding — directly relevant if you model manufacturing growth or depreciation.

lim(x→0) (e^x − 1)/x = 1

This is precisely why d/dx[e^x] = e^x — the unique property that makes e special. No other base has a growth rate equal to its own value at every point.

Foundation of e^x derivative

Derivative Rules

A derivative measures instantaneous rate of change. In manufacturing context: if f(t) = units produced, f'(t) = production rate. If f(x) = beam stress, f'(x) = rate of stress change along the beam.

Basic rules

d/dx [c] = 0 (constant) d/dx [xⁿ] = n·xⁿ⁻¹ (power rule) d/dx [c·f] = c·f' d/dx [f±g] = f' ± g'

Power rule is the workhorse — works for all real n, not just integers. Rewrite 1/x³ = x⁻³ and √x = x^(1/2) before applying it. This rewriting step is where most errors happen.

Rewrite before differentiating

Product & quotient rules

d/dx [f·g] = f'g + fg' d/dx [f/g] = (f'g − fg') / g²

"First times derivative of second, plus second times derivative of first." Quotient rule: "low dee-high minus high dee-low, over low-squared." Used for any x·sin(x) or tan(x)=sin/cos expression.

d/dx[x·sin(x)] = sin(x) + x·cos(x)
d/dx[tan x] = (cos²x+sin²x)/cos²x = sec²x

Chain rule ★

d/dx [f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

"Outer derivative times inner derivative." Nearly every real-world function is a composition — this rule is used more than any other. Master it first.

d/dx[sin(3x²+1)] = cos(3x²+1) · 6x
d/dx[e^(sin x)] = e^(sin x) · cos(x)
d/dx[(x²+1)^10] = 10(x²+1)^9 · 2x
Most used rule overall

Trig derivatives

d/dx [sin x] = cos x d/dx [cos x] = −sin x d/dx [tan x] = sec²x d/dx [cot x] = −csc²x d/dx [sec x] = sec x · tan x d/dx [csc x] = −csc x · cot x

sin and cos cycle: sin→cos→−sin→−cos. The negative signs appear in cos, cot, and csc derivatives. Tan/sec pair up; cot/csc pair up.

Exponential & log derivatives

d/dx [eˣ] = eˣ d/dx [aˣ] = aˣ · ln(a) d/dx [ln x] = 1/x d/dx [logₐx] = 1/(x·ln a)

e^x is its own derivative — unique to base e. The antiderivative of 1/x is ln|x|+C — you cannot get this from the power rule (x⁻¹ is the exception case). This integral appears constantly.

∫(1/x)dx = ln|x|+C, not power rule

Inverse trig derivatives

d/dx [arcsin x] = 1/√(1−x²) d/dx [arccos x] = −1/√(1−x²) d/dx [arctan x] = 1/(1+x²)

These appear as antiderivatives in Calc 2: ∫1/√(1−x²) dx = arcsin(x)+C and ∫1/(1+x²) dx = arctan(x)+C. The arctan form appears in partial fractions for irreducible quadratics.

Arctan in partial fractions

Implicit differentiation

Differentiate both sides, treat y as a function of x: d/dx[y] = dy/dx

For equations like x²+y²=r² (circle), you can't solve for y easily. Instead: 2x + 2y·(dy/dx) = 0, so dy/dx = −x/y. Used for related rates problems ("how fast is the radius changing?").

Related rates problems
x²+y²=25 → 2x+2y(dy/dx)=0 → dy/dx=−x/y

Logarithmic differentiation

y = f(x)^g(x) → take ln both sides ln y = g(x)·ln(f(x)) then differentiate implicitly

For complicated products or variable exponents. Example: y = x^x → ln y = x·ln x → (1/y)·y' = ln x + 1 → y' = x^x(ln x + 1). Also simplifies y = x²·sin(x)·e^x products.

Variable exponents, complex products

Algebra, Logs & Exponentials

These let you rewrite expressions into forms you can differentiate or integrate. Calculus professors expect fluency here — slow algebra is the #1 cause of errors on exams.

e^x and ln(x) are inverse functions — reflections across y=x

Exponent rules

aᵐ · aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ aᵐ / aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ a^(1/n) = ⁿ√a (ab)ⁿ = aⁿbⁿ

Rewrite before differentiating: 1/x³ → x⁻³, √x → x^(1/2), x²·x³ → x⁵. This is the most common algebraic manipulation in Calc 1.

Rewrite first, then apply power rule

Logarithm rules

ln(ab) = ln a + ln b ln(a/b) = ln a − ln b ln(aⁿ) = n·ln a ln(eˣ) = x and e^(ln x) = x ln(1) = 0 and ln(e) = 1

Take ln of both sides to simplify before differentiating complicated products like f = x²·sin(x)·e^x. Also used to evaluate limits (combine with L'Hôpital) and simplify integrands.

Logarithmic differentiation

Completing the square

ax²+bx+c = a(x+b/2a)² + (c−b²/4a)
x²+6x+5 → (x+3)²−4
x²+4x+7 → (x+2)²+3

Puts quadratics into the form (x−h)²+k², which matches the arctan formula ∫1/(x²+a²) dx = (1/a)arctan(x/a)+C. Critical for partial fractions when the denominator has irreducible quadratics.

Partial fractions, arctan integrals

Factoring patterns

a²−b² = (a+b)(a−b) a³−b³ = (a−b)(a²+ab+b²) a³+b³ = (a+b)(a²−ab+b²) (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b² (a−b)² = a²−2ab+b²

Difference of squares cancels factors in 0/0 limits. Rationalize by multiplying by conjugate (a+b) when you see a−b with radicals in a limit.

Canceling limit indeterminates

Quadratic formula

x = [−b ± √(b²−4ac)] / 2a Discriminant b²−4ac: > 0: two real roots = 0: one repeated root < 0: irreducible quadratic

Used in partial fraction decomposition to find roots of polynomial denominators. If b²−4ac < 0, you have irreducible quadratics — those need completing the square and then arctan.

Partial fraction decomposition

Polynomial long division

If deg(num) ≥ deg(denom): divide first!
(x³+x) ÷ (x²+1) = x + 0/(x²+1)
(x²+3x+5) ÷ (x+1) = x+2 + 3/(x+1)

Before partial fractions, check degrees. If numerator degree ≥ denominator degree, do long division first to get a proper fraction. Skipping this step corrupts everything after it.

Geometry & Coordinates

These formulas appear in optimization problems (max/min), related rates, and applications of integration (area, volume). Manufacturing context: minimizing material cost = constrained optimization.

Trig substitution — match the form under √ to the right triangle

Area formulas

Rectangle: A = l·w Triangle: A = ½·b·h Circle: A = πr² Trapezoid: A = ½(b₁+b₂)·h Ellipse: A = π·a·b

Optimization: "find dimensions maximizing area given fixed perimeter" — classic Calc 1 problem. Related rates: "how fast is circle area growing if radius grows at 2 cm/s?"

Optimization & related rates

Volume formulas

Box: V = l·w·h Cylinder: V = πr²h Cone: V = ⅓πr²h Sphere: V = (4/3)πr³

Volumes of revolution in Calc 2 integrate π[f(x)]² dx — summing circular cross-sections. The cylinder formula IS this integral for constant f(x). Manufacturing: tank design, material volume calculations.

Solids of revolution (Calc 2)

Surface area formulas

Cylinder: SA = 2πr² + 2πrh Sphere: SA = 4πr² Cone: SA = πr² + πrl (l=slant)

"What cylinder dimensions minimize surface area for a fixed volume?" — the can problem. You differentiate SA with a constraint from V=constant, set derivative to zero. Directly applicable in container/packaging manufacturing.

Constrained optimization

Distance, midpoint, slope

dist = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²] midpoint = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2) slope = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁) = Δy/Δx

The derivative IS the slope of a tangent line. The difference quotient [f(x+h)−f(x)]/h is exactly Δy/Δx as Δx→0. Geometric intuition helps you sanity-check derivative answers.

Geometric meaning of derivatives

Line equations

Slope-intercept: y = mx + b Point-slope: y−y₁ = m(x−x₁) General: ax + by + c = 0

Tangent line at (x₀, f(x₀)) uses point-slope with slope m=f'(x₀). Linear approximation: f(x)≈f(a)+f'(a)(x−a). This is Newton's method, Taylor series, and forward Euler all in disguise.

Tangent lines & linearization

Conic sections

Circle: (x−h)²+(y−k)² = r² Parabola: y = a(x−h)²+k Ellipse: (x−h)²/a² + (y−k)²/b² = 1

Trig substitution in Calc 2 is based on these shapes: √(r²−x²) → circle → use x=r sinθ; √(r²+x²) → use x=r tanθ; √(x²−r²) → use x=r secθ. Recognizing the form tells you which substitution to make.
