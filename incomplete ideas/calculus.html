The ε–δ Definition of a Limit

The limit says: limx→a f(x) = L means you can get f(x) as close as you want to L by keeping x close enough to a. The formal definition makes "close enough" precise.

Formal definition: For every ε > 0 (how close to L you demand), there exists δ > 0 such that: if 0 < |x − a| < δ, then |f(x) − L| < ε.

Think of it as a game: your enemy picks any ε (a tiny tolerance around L on the y-axis). You win by finding a δ (a tolerance around a on the x-axis) so that every x within δ of a maps to within ε of L. If you can always win, the limit exists.

Continuity

A function is continuous at a if three things hold simultaneously:

1. f(a) exists
2. limx→a f(x) exists
3. limx→a f(x) = f(a) The limit equals the actual value — no gaps, jumps, or holes.

Differentiability vs Continuity

Continuous means no breaks. Differentiable means the function has a well-defined slope — no sharp corners, vertical tangents, or cusps. Differentiable always implies continuous, but not vice versa.

Trigonometry — Geometric Roots

Everything in trig comes from the unit circle. A point on a circle of radius 1, at angle θ from the positive x-axis, has coordinates (cos θ, sin θ). That's the definition — not a ratio of triangle sides, but a point on a circle.

Unit circle — sin, cos, tan, all at once
40°

Why "tangent"? Draw a vertical line touching (tangent to) the circle at x=1. Extend the ray through the angle until it hits that line. The height of intersection is tan(θ). The name comes from this literal tangent line construction.

Addition Formulas — the geometry

The formula sin(A+B) = sinA cosB + cosA sinB isn't algebra magic — it's a geometric decomposition. Point P is on the unit circle at angle A+B. Drop a perpendicular to the intermediate radius (at angle A) and the vertical distance splits into two pieces.

d/dx sin x = cos x
d/dx cos x = −sin x
d/dx tan x = sec²x tan = sin/cos, apply quotient rule, then use sin²+cos²=1 → sec²x

Logarithms & Exponentials

The natural logarithm ln(x) asks: what power do I raise e to, to get x? Equivalently, ln(x) is the area under 1/t from 1 to x. This definition makes its derivative obvious: d/dx ln(x) = 1/x.

Logarithm Rules — why each works
Product rule
ln(ab) = ln a + ln b
Because areas add: area from 1→ab = area 1→a + scaled area 1→b
Quotient rule
ln(a/b) = ln a − ln b
Division undoes multiplication, so areas subtract
Power rule
ln(aⁿ) = n·ln a
Multiplying a by itself n times adds the area n times
Change of base
log_b(x) = ln x / ln b
Rescale the natural log by how "big" b is compared to e
Inverse relationship
e^(ln x) = x
ln(eˣ) = x
ln and exp are inverses — they undo each other
ln(1) and ln(e)

ln(e) = 1
