Chapter 1 — The Foundation

|x| is a distance,
not an operation

Every rule about absolute values follows immediately from one geometric fact. Once you see it, you never need to memorise a rule again.

The only thing you need to know
|x| is the distance from x to 0 on the number line.
|x − a| is the distance from x to a on the number line.

That is the complete definition. Everything else is a consequence.

The notation |x| looks like it means "remove the minus sign if there is one." That's a valid procedure but it's the wrong way to think about it. The moment you think of it as removing signs, you're in symbol-manipulation mode and you lose the picture.

Think of the number line as a ruler. |x| is how far x is from the zero mark — in either direction. Distance is always positive. Distance from 3 to 0 is 3. Distance from −3 to 0 is also 3. That's why |3| = |−3| = 3.

|x| as distance from zero — drag x
-3.0
|x − a| means "distance from x to a"

Now shift your zero point. Instead of measuring from 0, measure from some point a. The distance from x to a is |x − a|. This is the key to everything that follows.

|x − 2| is the distance from x to 2. That's it. When x = 5, the distance to 2 is 3, so |5 − 2| = 3. When x = −1, the distance to 2 is 3, so |−1 − 2| = |−3| = 3. Both points are the same distance from 2, just in opposite directions.

Simple inequalities — no rules needed

Now watch what happens to inequalities. |x − 2| < 3 in algebra says "the absolute value of x minus 2 is less than 3." In geometry it says something you can just see: "x is within distance 3 of 2."

All points within distance 3 of 2 live in the interval from 2−3 to 2+3, which is −1 to 5. You don't apply a rule. You draw a circle of radius 3 centered at 2 on the number line and read off the endpoints.

|x − a| < r means a − r < x < a + r "x is within distance r of a" — an interval centered at a with radius r
|x − a| > r means x < a − r OR x > a + r "x is more than distance r from a" — everything outside that interval

Chapter 2 — The Algebra Rules

Why the rules are obvious
from the picture

Every algebraic rule for absolute values is a geometric fact in disguise. Here is where each one comes from.

Factoring out constants — |ca| = |c||a|

Your specific question: why does |2x − 4| = 2|x − 2|? And why don't you flip anything?

The geometric answer: |2x − 4| = |2(x − 2)| = |2| · |x − 2| = 2|x − 2|. Multiplying by 2 stretches all distances by a factor of 2. If x is distance d from 2, then 2x is distance 2d from 4. The factor comes out as its absolute value because distances are always positive — if you multiplied by −2, distances still scale by 2 (the negative just reflects, it doesn't shrink).

Why |−2| = 2, not −2
Multiplying by −2 reflects the number line and stretches it by 2. The stretch factor is 2. The reflection doesn't change distances, it just flips direction. So |−2x| = 2|x|, not −2|x|. A distance can never be negative.
Worked example 2.1 — your exact question
Simplify |2x − 4|. Then solve |2x − 4| < 6.
1
Factor out the constant from inside
|2x − 4| = |2(x − 2)| = |2| · |x − 2| = 2|x − 2|
We factored 2 from both terms: 2x−4 = 2(x−2). Then |2|·|x−2| = 2|x−2|.
2
Substitute back into the inequality
2|x − 2| < 6
3
Divide both sides by 2 — no sign flip because 2 is positive
|x − 2| < 3
Dividing by a positive number never changes the direction of an inequality. 2 is positive. No flip.
4
Now it's the simple form: x within distance 3 of 2
2 − 3 < x < 2 + 3 → −1 < x < 5
x ∈ (−1, 5). Geometrically: all points within distance 3 of 2.
The sign-flip rule — when and exactly why

The sign flips when you divide or multiply both sides of an inequality by a negative number. This has nothing to do with absolute values specifically — it's a general inequality rule. But it's worth understanding from the picture so you never misapply it.

Why dividing by a negative flips the inequality
On the number line, multiplying by −1 reflects everything through zero. 3 > 1, but after reflecting: −3 < −1. The larger number becomes the smaller one because it ends up further left. So > becomes < and vice versa. This always happens with negative multipliers.
Inequality flip — multiply by negative and watch what happens
-2.0

The rule in absolute value problems: You will rarely need to flip a sign when working with |x − a| < r, because you reduce everything to that form first. The flip happens earlier — when you divide out a negative coefficient to get to the standard form. Example: |−3x + 6| = 3|x − 2|. No flip needed inside the absolute value. Once it's |x − 2| < something, you just do centre ± radius.

Worked example 2.2 — negative coefficient
Solve |−3x + 6| < 9.
1
Factor out the coefficient
|−3x + 6| = |−3(x − 2)| = |−3| · |x − 2| = 3|x − 2|
|−3| = 3, not −3. The absolute value makes it positive. No sign flip here.
2
Divide both sides by 3 (positive — no flip)
3|x − 2| < 9 → |x − 2| < 3
3
Centre ± radius
2 − 3 < x < 2 + 3 → −1 < x < 5
Same answer as before — |2x−4| and |−3x+6| produce the same interval here because both reduce to |x−2|.
Splitting into cases — when the centre isn't obvious

Sometimes you can't factor cleanly, or the expression inside isn't linear. Then you split into cases based on whether the inside is positive or negative. This is just saying: a distance equals d means you went d to the right OR d to the left.

|f(x)| = d means f(x) = d OR f(x) = −d Two cases: the inside was positive (you just remove the bars) or negative (you flip the sign)

Solve |x² − 4| < 5.

Write as a double inequality (the standard form for <)
−5 < x² − 4 < 5
|something| < 5 means that something lives in (−5, 5). Split into two inequalities.

Add 4 to all three parts

Left side: x² > −1 is always true (squares are never negative)
x² > −1 → always satisfied, ignore
4
Right side: x² < 9 means |x| < 3
x² < 9 → |x| < 3 → −3 < x < 3

Chapter 3 — Calculus Applications

The inequalities calculus actually needs

Every ε–δ proof is a chain of absolute value manipulations. Here is every move you will need, with the geometric picture for each one.

The ε–δ manipulations

When you write a limit proof, you are doing one thing: starting from |x − a| < δ (you're close to a on the x-axis) and ending with |f(x) − L| < ε (therefore you're close to L on the y-axis). Every step in between is an inequality manipulation.

The manipulations you actually need, in order of how often they appear:

1. Factoring: |cx − ca| = |c| · |x − a| Factor the constant out, absolute value makes it positive
2. Dividing: if |x−a| < δ and δ = ε/c, then c|x−a| < ε Multiply both sides by c (positive) — no flip
3. Bounding: if |x−a| < 1, then we can bound |x+a| ≤ M Assume δ ≤ 1 first to get a handle on variable factors
4. Triangle ineq: |A + B| ≤ |A| + |B| The length of a sum ≤ sum of lengths — splitting complex expressions
Worked example 3.1 — the full chain for a linear function
Prove limx→3 (5x − 2) = 13. Show every inequality step explicitly.
S
Scratch: what do we need to manipulate?
|f(x) − L| = |(5x − 2) − 13| = |5x − 15| = |5(x − 3)| = 5|x − 3|
We need 5|x−3| < ε. We control |x−3| < δ. So 5|x−3| < 5δ. Need 5δ ≤ ε → choose δ = ε/5.
1
Formal proof — start from assumption
Given ε > 0. Choose δ = ε/5.
Assume 0 < |x − 3| < δ.
2
Chain of inequalities
|(5x − 2) − 13|
= |5x − 15|
= |5| · |x − 3|   ← factor out 5
= 5|x − 3|
< 5 · δ   ← use |x−3| < δ
= 5 · (ε/5)
= ε ✓
Every line is one move: factor, substitute the bound, simplify. That is all ε–δ proofs are.
Worked example 3.2 — quadratic, needs bounding
Prove limx→1 x² = 1. Why do we need the min(1, ε/3) trick?
S
Scratch: factor |x² − 1|
|x² − 1| = |(x−1)(x+1)| = |x−1| · |x+1|
We control |x−1| (it's less than δ). But |x+1| contains x, which we don't fully control. We need to bound it.
1
Assume δ ≤ 1 to cage x near 1
If |x − 1| < 1, then 0 < x < 2
So 1 < x + 1 < 3
Therefore |x + 1| < 3
We picked 1 as a convenient cage. Could pick any number; 1 is clean.
2
Now chain the inequalities
|x² − 1| = |x−1| · |x+1| < δ · 3 = 3δ
We need 3δ < ε, so δ < ε/3.
3
Choose δ satisfying BOTH constraints: δ ≤ 1 AND δ ≤ ε/3
δ = min(1, ε/3)
The min ensures both conditions hold simultaneously. If ε is large (say ε=100), then ε/3=33 but δ=1 keeps us caged. If ε is tiny (say ε=0.01), then ε/3=0.003 is the binding constraint.
4
Formal proof chain
|x² − 1| = |x−1|·|x+1| < δ · 3 ≤ (ε/3) · 3 = ε ✓
The min(1, ε/M) pattern appears in every quadratic or higher-degree ε–δ proof. Recognise it as "I need δ small enough to both cage the variable factor AND produce ε."

The triangle inequality — why it's called that

The triangle inequality says |A + B| ≤ |A| + |B|. The name comes from geometry: in any triangle, one side is always shorter than the sum of the other two. On the number line, the distance from 0 to A+B is at most the distance from 0 to A plus the distance from 0 to B, because going via the middle is never shorter than going direct.

When you use it in calculus
When you have |f(x) − L| and you need to split it into manageable pieces. You write f(x) − L = (f(x) − f(a)) + (f(a) − L) and then use the triangle inequality to bound each piece separately. It lets you break hard problems into two easier ones.
|A + B| ≤ |A| + |B| (triangle inequality)
||A| − |B|| ≤ |A − B| (reverse triangle inequality) The reverse form says the difference of distances ≤ the distance between points
Chaining inequalities — the full pattern

Most ε–δ proofs and most inequality problems in calculus involve a chain: you start with something and bound it step by step until you reach ε. Every link in the chain is one of three moves:

Move 1 — Algebra: Factor, expand, simplify. |5x − 15| = 5|x − 3|.
Move 2 — Substitute a bound: If |x−3| < δ, replace it: 5|x−3| < 5δ.
Move 3 — Choose δ to finish: Set 5δ = ε, so δ = ε/5.

Worked example 3.3 — full chain, harder case
In a proof that limx→2 (x² + 3x) = 10, show the complete chain once δ = min(1, ε/8) is chosen.
S
Scratch: factor |(x²+3x) − 10|
|(x²+3x) − 10| = |x² + 3x − 10| = |(x−2)(x+5)| = |x−2|·|x+5|
1
Bound |x+5| using |x−2| < 1
|x−2| < 1 → 1 < x < 3 → 6 < x+5 < 8 → |x+5| < 8
2
Choose δ = min(1, ε/8) and write the chain
|(x²+3x)−10|
= |x−2| · |x+5|
< δ · 8   ← |x−2|<δ, |x+5|<8
≤ (ε/8) · 8   ← δ ≤ ε/8
= ε ✓
The pattern is always: factor → bound the variable part → choose δ → chain to ε.
