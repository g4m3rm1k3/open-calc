Fractions — Part 1

Compound fractions
fraction inside a fraction

This appears constantly in calculus — the difference quotient produces them, and limits of rational functions need them. The trick is always the same: multiply top and bottom by the LCD of everything inside.

The Core Trick
Multiply top and bottom by the LCD of the inner denominators
When you see a fraction whose numerator or denominator contains fractions, find the lowest common denominator of all the inner fractions and multiply the whole thing (top AND bottom) by it. This is legal because you're multiplying by 1.
Simplify: (1/x − 1/a) / (x − a) Step 0 of 6
Verify it — plug in numbers
Try x = 5, a = 2. Left side: (1/5 − 1/2) / (5 − 2) = (2/10 − 5/10) / 3 = (−3/10) / 3 = −1/10.
Right side (simplified): −1/(xa) = −1/(5×2) = −1/10. ✓ Same.

Why it matters in calculus: This exact form appears when you compute limx→a (1/x − 1/a)/(x−a) = f′(a) for f(x) = 1/x.
Try your own values — confirm (1/x − 1/a)/(x−a) = −1/(xa)
Fractions — Part 2

Adding fractions
with variable denominators

The rule is identical to numeric fractions — find a common denominator, convert each fraction, add. The only trap is forgetting to distribute the numerator correctly after converting.

Combine: 1/(x+h) − 1/x, then factor Step 0 of 5
Why this matters — the derivative of 1/x
The difference quotient for f(x) = 1/x is [1/(x+h) − 1/x] / h.
After the steps above: the numerator becomes −h / [x(x+h)].
Divide by h: −1 / [x(x+h)]. As h→0: −1/x².
Every step is just fraction addition with variable denominators — no magic.
Verify: 1/(x+h) − 1/x = −h / [x(x+h)]
Fractions — Part 3

Cancelling factors
the move that makes limits work

Most limit problems that "look like 0/0" are fixed by factoring the numerator and denominator, then cancelling a common factor. The key: you can only cancel factors (things multiplied), never terms (things added).

The trap everyone falls into: (x² + 3x) / x ≠ x² + 3. You CANNOT cancel the x in the denominator with just the x² in the numerator — x is a term here, not a factor of the whole numerator. But (x² + 3x) / x = x(x+3)/x = x+3. Factor first, THEN cancel.

Simplify (x² − 4) / (x − 2) — a limit that looks like 0/0 Step 0 of 4
Why (x²−4)/(x−2) and (x+2) are the same — except at x=2
3.00
Verify: (x²−4)/(x−2) = x+2 for x ≠ 2
Factoring — Part 1

Difference of squares
and why it's everywhere

a² − b² = (a+b)(a−b). You probably know this formula, but the visual proof makes it unforgettable — and it works with any expression in place of a and b, including square roots.

Extension — works with square roots too: √x − √a = (x−a) / (√x + √a). This is just difference of squares with A=√x, B=√a: A²−B² = (A+B)(A−B), so A−B = (A²−B²)/(A+B) = (x−a)/(√x+√a). Used constantly in rationalizing limits.

Factoring — Part 2

Factoring to cancel
the core limit technique

When a limit gives 0/0, there is ALWAYS a shared factor causing both the numerator and denominator to be zero. Find it, cancel it, and the limit becomes evaluable. Here's the systematic method.

The 0/0 detection method
If plugging x=a into f(x)/g(x) gives 0/0, then (x−a) is a factor of BOTH f and g.
Why? If f(a)=0, then by the Factor Theorem, (x−a) divides f(x). So always try dividing both by (x−a) — either by factoring or by polynomial long division.
Compute lim(x→2) of (x³ − 8) / (x² − 4) Step 0 of 6
Verify: (x³−8)/(x²−4) → check limit = 3/2 as x approaches 2
(try values close to 2)
Factoring — Part 3

Completing the square
the Babylonian method, geometrically

This is the one technique that actually is geometric — the Babylonians did it by moving physical dirt. Seeing the geometry makes the algebra obvious and memorable.

Why x² + bx = (x + b/2)² − (b/2)² — animated geometry
4.0
3.0
Complete the square: x² + 6x + 5 = 0 Step 0 of 6
Geometric proof
x² + bx is the area of an x×x square PLUS an x×b rectangle.
Cut the rectangle in half → two strips of x×(b/2). Attach one to the bottom, one to the right.
Now it almost makes a larger square of side (x + b/2), but the corner (b/2)×(b/2) is missing.
So: x² + bx = (x + b/2)² − (b/2)². That's completing the square.
Radical Tricks — Part 1

The conjugate trick
eliminating square roots from limits

When a limit has √(something) − √(something else) in the numerator or denominator, multiply top and bottom by the conjugate. This uses difference of squares to clear the radical.

The conjugate trick
The conjugate of (√A − √B) is (√A + √B).
(√A − √B)(√A + √B) = A − B No more square roots in that product — just the difference of the things under the radical.
Compute lim(x→0) of (√(x+4) − 2) / x Step 0 of 6
Verify: (√(x+4) − 2)/x → 1/4 as x → 0
(try 0.1, 0.01, 0.001)
Radical Tricks — Part 2

Rationalizing denominators
and the cube root conjugate

For cube roots the conjugate changes. The identity a³−b³ = (a−b)(a²+ab+b²) is the tool. With A=∛x and B=∛a: x−a = (∛x−∛a)(∛x²+∛(xa)+∛a²).

Compute lim(x→8) of (∛x − 2) / (x − 8) Step 0 of 5
Verify: (∛x − 2)/(x − 8) → 1/12 as x → 8
Calculus Moves — Part 1

The algebra inside limits
recognising which technique to use

When you see a limit that gives 0/0, there is a decision tree. The technique depends on the form of the expression. Here it is explicitly.

Decision tree — 0/0 forms
Which algebraic move to use
Both polynomial? Factor both and cancel the common (x−a) factor.
Has √ in numerator? Multiply by conjugate of the radical expression.
Has 1/x or 1/(x+h) style fractions? Find LCD and combine into a single fraction first.
Has ∛ or higher root? Use the sum/difference of cubes identity to create a factor to cancel.
Has eˣ or ln? Usually needs L'Hôpital or substitution — that's a later topic.
A harder one: lim(x→1) of (x³ − x² − x + 1) / (x² − 2x + 1) Step 0 of 7
Verify: (x³−x²−x+1)/(x²−2x+1) → −2 as x → 1
Calculus Moves — Part 2

Inequality chains
the moves inside ε–δ proofs

The algebra inside an ε–δ proof is a chain of inequalities. Each step is one of three moves. Once you see the pattern, every proof is the same structure.

Scratch work: find δ for lim(x→3) x² = 9, given ε Step 0 of 7

The one algebraic move that surprises people: If |A·B| < δ·M and we want this < ε, we need δ < ε/M. So we choose δ = ε/M. This is just: if you know the product is bounded by δ·M, set δ·M = ε and solve for δ. Pure algebra, but it looks mysterious in a proof until you see it written out.

Inequalities

Inequality moves
that work differently than equations

Inequalities follow the same rules as equations except for two things: multiplying/dividing by a negative flips the sign, and you cannot multiply both sides by a variable unless you know its sign.

Solve: −3 < (2x − 1)/4 ≤ 5, then interpret geometrically Step 0 of 5
The sign trap — multiplying by a variable
You CANNOT do: from 1/x < 3, multiply both sides by x to get 1 < 3x.
If x > 0: 1 < 3x ✓ (sign preserved) If x < 0: 1 > 3x ✗ (you needed to flip!) Solution: always split into cases x>0 and x<0, or use |x| reasoning.
