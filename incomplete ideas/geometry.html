Most calculus confusion traces back to five or six geometric facts that were either taught as rules to memorize, or skipped entirely. This page builds each one from scratch with visual proofs, interactive diagrams, and the connection to where it appears in calculus.

How to use this: Drag every slider. When a formula appears in calculus later, come back to the picture — the formula should feel like a description of something you have already seen, not a new rule to memorize.

Angles — Part 1

Parallel lines and the angle relationships

When a line crosses two parallel lines it creates angles. Three relationships emerge that feel like separate rules but are all one idea: parallel lines are copies of each other, so any crossing line hits them at the same angle.

The one idea behind all three rules
Parallel lines have identical direction. Any transversal hitting them hits both at the same angle. Same position on each line gives corresponding angles (equal). Opposite sides give alternate interior angles (equal). Same side between the lines gives co-interior angles (sum to 180°).

Why this matters for calculus
Similar triangle proofs — used in related rates and trig derivative proofs — depend on alternate interior angles. When two lines are parallel, equal angles let you prove triangles are similar, which gives equal ratios. If the angle rules feel arbitrary, similar triangle arguments have no foundation.

Angles — Part 3

The exterior angle theorem

An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles. This appears in circle theorem proofs and some calculus problems. It falls straight out of the angle sum theorem.

Triangles — Part 1

Similar triangles
the engine of proportion

Two triangles are similar if they have the same angles. Their sides are then in proportion. This is the most used geometric fact in calculus and the most often skimmed over.

Why the ratios are equal
Similar triangles are scale copies. Doubling all sides keeps angles the same and keeps every ratio of sides the same. The ratio is the shape — it does not depend on size. This is why tan theta = opposite / hypotenuse works for ANY right triangle with angle theta, regardless of size.

The AA rule: If two triangles share two angles, the third is forced (all three sum to 180), so all three match. You only need to show TWO angles are equal to prove similarity. In most proofs you find one shared angle and one from parallel lines — done.

Pythagoras — the square rearrangement proof

a squared plus b squared equals c squared. The proof by rearrangement shows both sides as the same leftover area when four identical triangles are arranged two different ways inside a big square.

Circles — Part 1

Radians are actual lengths

Radians are not just another way to measure angles. They are the ratio of arc length to radius. This makes them dimensionless but geometrically concrete — and it is why all calculus trig derivatives work out cleanly.

The definition
1 radian = the angle where the arc length equals the radius. If radius is r and arc has length s, the angle in radians is theta = s divided by r. A full circle has circumference 2 pi r so the full angle is 2 pi r divided by r = 2 pi radians = 360 degrees. The derivative formula d/dx of sin x = cos x only holds when x is in radians because the limit sin(h)/h approaches 1 only when h measures arc length divided by radius.

Why this matters for d/dx sin x = cos x
The key limit is lim h to 0 of sin(h)/h = 1. This is ONLY true when h is in radians. Geometrically: for small h in radians, the arc length h is approximately the chord length sin(h) since they both describe the same tiny gap on the unit circle. In degrees, sin(h degrees) is approximately pi times h divided by 180, so the ratio goes to pi/180, not 1. The formula d/dx sin x = cos x silently assumes radians.

Circles — Part 3

The inscribed angle theorem
why a semicircle gives 90 degrees

An angle inscribed in a circle is half the central angle subtending the same arc. The special case: an angle inscribed in a semicircle is always exactly 90 degrees. This appears in constructions and some optimization problems.

For Calculus — Part 3

Area formulas built from rings — connecting to integration

The area of a circle can be found by cutting it into thin rings and unwrapping them into a triangle. This is literally what the integral does. Seeing it geometrically makes integration feel obvious rather than mysterious.

The connection to integration
Each ring at radius r has circumference 2 pi r and tiny thickness dr.
Area of one ring is approximately 2 pi r times dr.
Total area = integral from 0 to R of 2 pi r dr = 2 pi times [r squared over 2] from 0 to R = pi R squared.
The picture of unwrapping rings into a triangle is literally what that integral is computing.
